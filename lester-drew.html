Vessels of honesty, Custodians of Legacy

The topography of a man’s face is a cartography of his journey; each wrinkle is not a mark of decay, but a sacred inscription of struggle, resilience, and hard-won wisdom. The depth in his words is the echo from his personal battleground, a place where character is both shattered and forged anew. It is said a man is ultimately judged not by his station, but by his valour.

Lester Drew is the living embodiment of this truth.

Our meeting was no accident of fate. Lester had been a quiet witness to our cultural work long before our formal introduction. After myself, my father, and the Imanzi dance group had performed for the Rotary Club, his support was not merely polite applause—it was a genuine, actionable belief in preserving the soul of our community. It was Lester who, with unwavering conviction, facilitated funding from the Rotarians, empowering us to acquire the equipment we needed. His mission became our mission: to help my father and me heal and unite our community through the ancient languages of dance, music, and shared workshops.

I believe the divine often speaks not from the heavens, but through the hearts of people. Lester is truly one of those vessels. In my life, I have prayed for a brother, a comrade of such unwavering principle. He appeared during the roughest throes of cultural shock in Australia, a guiding light in a disorienting new world. It is these rare souls who see us not for our origins, but for our potential, and who stand beside us in our darkest hour.

His face is a rich landscape, a library of kindness holding a firm belief in truth and charity. He embodies the conviction my father often voiced: “The truth can walk through fire and never be burned.” Interviewing Lester felt less like a discovery and more like a confirmation of what my spirit already knew. That this path, this “Rugamba”—this battlefield of life—is one from which I may not return unscathed. But if this is a fight to the death for what is right, then I will consider it the highest honor to fall alongside men who know “Rurema,” their Creator, and who wage war with love.

To know truth is to know honor. I have chosen to live by this idea. I will stand by it even if the ship sinks, for some principles are worth the fall. And as Sir Lester Drew always reminds me with his serene certainty, “We have already won the battle.” The victory lies in the choosing, in the standing, in the unwavering commitment to what is good.

This is the lesson I will impart to the youth: that men are not forged by blood alone; we are not merely fists and rage. We are vessels of truth, custodians of legacy. We are the final stand upon which people will base their belief in goodness, in charity, and in the unwavering power of a righteous cause.

-Eric Kagorora
